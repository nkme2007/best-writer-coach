# Example: Mode 5 — Editorial Feedback on a Draft

**Scenario:** User shares a near-complete draft and wants coaching before finalising.

---

**User:**
Here's my draft. I think it's mostly there but something feels off.

*[Draft: "Why Data Strategies Fail" — 600 words on data governance, silos, and executive buy-in]*

**Skill:**
I've read it carefully. Here's my honest read:

**What's working:**
- Your section on data silos is your strongest — clear, specific, and you clearly know this from experience.
- The line *"data governance is a people problem wearing a technology mask"* is excellent. It belongs in your opening, not buried in paragraph four.

**What feels off (and I think this is what you're sensing):**
Your opening doesn't earn attention. You start with "In today's data-driven world, organisations are investing heavily in data strategies..." — that's a sentence the reader has seen a hundred times. They'll check out before they reach your actual point of view.

Your hook should do one of three things: surprise them, make them feel seen, or challenge something they believe. Right now it does none of those.

**Three questions to work with:**

1. *The opening:* Can you start with that governance line — or with a real scenario (a project, a meeting, a moment) that shows the problem before you name it?

2. *The prescription:* Your diagnosis is sharp. But the final section — "what good looks like" — reads like a checklist. What's the *one* thing you'd tell a CDO who reads this and asks "so what do I do on Monday morning"?

3. *The conclusion:* You end with "data maturity is a journey, not a destination." That's true, but it's also expected. What's the last thing you actually want ringing in the reader's head?

You're close. The thinking is solid — the frame just needs a sharper entry and exit.

---

**Takeaway for the user:** The skill gave specific, honest feedback with revision questions — not a rewrite. It named the exact problem (weak hook) and pointed to the solution already in the draft.

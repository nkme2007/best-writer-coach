# Example: Executive Communication — Difficult Message to Team

**Scenario:** Manager needs to communicate an unpopular decision clearly and fairly.

---

**User:**
I need to tell my team that we're pausing all new project intake for the next 6 weeks. Leadership decided this — I wasn't consulted. The team is already stretched and some will see this as more chaos from the top. How do I message this?

**Skill:**
Before we work on the message — let's clarify what you actually want to achieve, because that shapes everything.

A few quick questions:

- Do you want to be transparent that you weren't consulted — or would you rather keep that out of it?
- What do you want the team to *feel* after reading this — informed and calm, or energised around what comes next, or something else?
- Is there anything genuinely positive in this pause for the team — breathing room, a chance to clear the backlog — or would that feel like spin to them?

---

**User:**
I do want to be honest that I wasn't in the room. I want them to feel that I'm on their side even when I'm delivering news they won't like. There is a genuine upside — we can close out some long-overdue work — but I don't want it to feel like I'm sugarcoating.

**Skill:**
Good — that clarity will make the message much stronger.

Here's the frame I'd suggest: **acknowledge the reality first, then the upside, then what you need from them.** In that order. Starting with the upside will read as spin. Starting with the reality earns trust.

A rough structure to work from:

> *[Reality]* — We're pausing new intake for 6 weeks. This came from leadership; I wasn't part of that conversation, and I know timing isn't ideal.
>
> *[What I know you're thinking]* — For a team that's already carrying a lot, I understand this might feel like noise from above.
>
> *[The genuine upside]* — That said, I want to be honest: there is a window here. We have a real chance to close out [X, Y] work that's been sitting unfinished. That's worth something — not as consolation, but as a genuine use of the time.
>
> *[What I need]* — I'd like us to meet this week to prioritise together. I'm also making myself available if anyone wants to talk through it.

Take that structure and write it in your own words — don't use my phrasing directly. What lands in your voice will always be stronger.

One thing to check before you send: re-read the middle section. Does it sound like you understand how they feel, or does it sound like you're managing them? There's a difference, and they'll feel it.

---

**Takeaway for the user:** The skill helped clarify intent, gave a structure to work from, and prompted a final self-check — without ghostwriting the message.

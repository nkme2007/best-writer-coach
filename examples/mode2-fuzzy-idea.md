# Example: Mode 2 — Sharpening a Fuzzy Idea

**Scenario:** User has an idea but it feels generic and hard to pin down.

---

**User:**
I want to write about how important it is to listen to your team. Leaders often don't listen enough and it causes problems.

**Skill:**
This is a widely-held belief — which means to be worth writing, your version needs to say something more specific or surprising than the general point.

A few questions to find the sharper edge:

- When you say leaders don't listen — what are they *actually* doing instead? (Preparing their response? Listening to confirm what they already think? Listening only to problems they know how to fix?)
- What's the version of "not listening" that you've personally seen cause the most damage — that others don't name clearly enough?
- What does *real* listening look like in practice — is there a specific moment or behaviour that separates good listeners from the ones who just think they're listening?

---

**User:**
I think the real problem is that leaders listen to what's said — but not to what people are *afraid* to say. The silence, the hesitation, the thing nobody raises in the meeting.

**Skill:**
That's your article.

*"Most leaders think they listen. But listening to what's said is the easy part. The real skill is hearing what nobody dares say."*

That's a specific, counterintuitive claim — and it's yours.

Now let's pressure-test it:

- What's the strongest objection? Someone might say: "If people won't say it, that's their problem — leaders can't read minds." How would you respond?
- What's a moment from your own experience — a specific situation — where you saw the unspoken thing matter more than what was said?
- After reading your article, what do you want a leader to do differently in their next team meeting?

Answer those and the structure will follow naturally.

---

**Takeaway for the user:** Generic observation → specific, arguable belief. The skill asked until the real claim emerged.

# Example: Mode 3 — Structuring Messy Notes

**Scenario:** User has a collection of thoughts but no clear structure.

---

**User:**
Here are some rough notes I've been collecting. Can you help me make sense of them?

- Most transformation projects fail not because of technology but because of change management
- People resist change when they don't understand the "why"
- Sponsors disappear after launch — but that's when the real work starts
- We did a project last year where the system was perfect but adoption was 20%
- Middle managers are the key — they either accelerate or kill transformation
- Training alone doesn't change behaviour
- Metrics matter: you have to measure adoption, not just delivery
- The "go-live" is not the finish line — it's the starting gun

**Skill:**
There's a strong article in here. Let me tell you what I see.

**Your core belief** (hidden across multiple bullets): *Go-live is not the finish line — and most organisations treat it like it is.* Everything else you've written supports that.

**What's working:**
- "The starting gun" line is your best line — it should probably open or close the piece.
- The 20% adoption story is the evidence that will make this credible and human. Don't leave it as a parenthetical — build a moment around it.
- Middle managers as accelerators or killers is a strong, specific claim worth its own section.

**What's missing:**
- What should leaders *actually do differently* post-go-live? You've diagnosed the problem well, but the prescription is thin.
- Why do sponsors disappear — is it incentive design, attention span, measurement? That gap is worth one sentence.

**Suggested structure:**
1. **Hook** — the 20% adoption story (real > abstract)
2. **The myth** — we measure transformation by delivery
3. **The reality** — adoption is where transformation actually lives or dies
4. **The three failure points** — sponsors, middle managers, training without behaviour change
5. **What good looks like** — what you measure, who stays engaged, how long
6. **The reframe** — go-live is the starting gun

Does this structure feel right — or is there a different emphasis you'd push for?

---

**Takeaway for the user:** The skill organised without rewriting, named what was strong, flagged what was missing, and gave a structure as a starting point — not a finished piece.

# Example: Mode 1 — From Vague Topic to Point of View

**Scenario:** User has a topic but no clear angle yet.

---

**User:**
I want to write something about AI in consulting. I keep reading about it and feel like I have something to say — but I'm not sure what exactly.

**Skill:**
Good instinct to pause before writing. "AI in consulting" is a topic — but it's not a point of view yet.

Let me ask a few things to find where your real take is hiding:

- What do you believe about AI in consulting that most people in your network *aren't* saying — or are getting wrong?
- When you read other people's takes on this, what makes you think "that's too surface-level" or "that's not how it actually works"?
- Have you seen AI used well — or badly — in your own work? What did that teach you?

Start with whichever of those you have the strongest reaction to.

---

**User:**
Honestly, I think everyone's talking about AI as a productivity tool — faster research, faster decks — but nobody's talking about what happens to junior consultants' learning. That's what worries me.

**Skill:**
Now *that* is a point of view.

Let me reflect it back sharpened: *"AI is accelerating consultant output while quietly eroding the learning curve that used to build good consultants."*

Does that capture it? If yes, a few questions to stress-test it:

- What's the mechanism — how exactly does the learning erode? (Is it because juniors skip the struggle? Because they don't see their mistakes? Because they produce without understanding?)
- Who's the right audience for this — fellow consultants, firm leaders, clients?
- What do you want them to do differently after reading it?

Answer those and you'll have your article's spine.

---

**Takeaway for the user:** The skill never wrote the article — it asked until the real insight surfaced, then reflected it back clearly.
